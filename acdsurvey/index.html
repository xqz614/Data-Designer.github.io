Abstract

Clinical dialogue represents a complex duality requiring both the empathetic fluency of natural conversation and the rigorous precision of evidence-based medicine. While Large Language Models possess unprecedented linguistic capabilities, their architectural reliance on reactive and stateless processing often favors probabilistic plausibility over factual veracity. This structural limitation has catalyzed a paradigm shift in medical AI from generative text prediction to agentic autonomy, where the model functions as a central reasoning engine capable of deliberate planning and persistent memory.

This survey provides a first-principles analysis of the cognitive architecture underpinning the shift from generative text prediction to agentic autonomy in clinical dialogue, introducing a novel taxonomy along the axes of knowledge source and agency objective.

Moving beyond existing reviews that primarily catalog downstream applications, we introduce a novel taxonomy structured along the orthogonal axes of knowledge source and agency objective to delineate the provenance of clinical knowledge against the system's operational scope. This framework facilitates a systematic analysis of the intrinsic trade-offs between creativity and reliability by categorizing methods into four archetypes: Latent Space Clinicians, Emergent Planners, Grounded Synthesizers, and Verifiable Workflow Automators. For each paradigm, we deconstruct the technical realization across the entire cognitive pipeline, encompassing strategic planning, memory management, action execution, collaboration, and evolution to reveal how distinct architectural choices balance the tension between autonomy and safety.

Four Agentic Paradigms

We categorize existing methods into four archetypes based on two orthogonal axes: the knowledge source (implicit knowledge navigation vs. explicit knowledge grounding) and the agency objective (event cognition vs. goal execution). Each paradigm represents a distinct philosophy for balancing the fundamental tension between autonomy and safety in clinical AI systems.

🤖
LSC
Latent Space Clinicians

Leverage the LLM's vast internal knowledge for creative synthesis and forming a coherent understanding of clinical situations. Trust the model's emergent reasoning capabilities to function like an experienced clinical assistant.

🧠
EP
Emergent Planners

Grant the LLM a high degree of autonomy, allowing it to dynamically devise multi-step plans to achieve complex clinical goals. The agent's behavior is emergent, independently determining necessary steps.

🔍
GS
Grounded Synthesizers

Operate under the principle that LLMs should function as powerful natural language interfaces to reliable external information, retrieving, integrating, and accurately summarizing from verifiable sources.

✅
VWA
Verifiable Workflow Automators

Agent autonomy is strictly constrained within pre-defined, verifiable clinical workflows or decision trees, ensuring maximum safety and predictability through structured processes.

Our proposed taxonomy framework structured along the orthogonal axes of knowledge source and agency objective. Each quadrant represents a distinct agentic paradigm with its cognitive pipeline components.

Key Contributions

1

Novel Taxonomy Framework

We introduce a novel taxonomy structured along the orthogonal axes of knowledge source and agency objective, categorizing methods into four distinct archetypes that capture the fundamental design trade-offs in clinical AI agents.

2

Comprehensive Cognitive Pipeline Analysis

For each paradigm, we provide an in-depth deconstruction of the technical realization across the entire cognitive pipeline: strategic planning, memory management, action execution, collaboration, and evolution.

3

Bridging Theory and Practice

We bridge abstract design philosophies with the pragmatic implementation ecosystem by mapping real-world applications to our taxonomy and systematically reviewing benchmarks and evaluation metrics specific to clinical agents.

Challenges & Future Directions

We identify four core challenge areas for the future of agentic clinical dialogue: Neuro-Symbolic Cognitive Architecture for integrating structured medical knowledge with neural reasoning; Holistic Patient Management for lifelong memory, sociolinguistic adaptation, and dynamic digital models; Human-AI Teaming for heterogeneous agent ecosystems and cultural competence; and High-Stakes Control for error recovery, clinical ethics, and safety guardrails.

A structured overview of open challenges and future research directions for agentic clinical dialogue, organized around four core themes.

Looking Forward

Furthermore, we bridge abstract design philosophies with the pragmatic implementation ecosystem. By mapping real-world applications to our taxonomy and systematically reviewing benchmarks and evaluation metrics specific to clinical agents, we provide a comprehensive reference for future development. We believe that the agentic paradigm is still a largely unexplored area in clinical dialogue, and we hope this survey and the accompanying resource repository will provide valuable insights for the research community.

We believe the future of clinical AI lies in the principled integration of agentic capabilities — planning, memory, tool use, and collaboration — with the rigorous safety requirements of healthcare, moving beyond reactive text generation toward truly autonomous clinical reasoning.
